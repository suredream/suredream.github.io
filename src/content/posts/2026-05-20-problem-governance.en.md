Most people start learning work through pattern matching. They see a task, match it to a familiar template, and execute the known move.

That is necessary, but it is not sufficient for independent work.

Independent work requires the ability to frame the problem space, define scope, align stakeholders, execute in small loops, and continuously validate whether the work is still solving the right problem.

The jump from junior to senior capability is not just about doing harder tasks. It is about moving from task execution to problem governance.

## The Minimal Model

Work capability can be modeled as three linked abilities:

1. Problem-space modeling: seeing the real goal, boundary, dependency, and risk.
2. Execution with validation: turning action into feedback, not just output.
3. Organizational communication: helping others make decisions with lower cognitive cost.

A person becomes more independent when they can connect these three layers.

## Upward Management

Upward management starts from a simple premise: every manager has an attention model. Their time, risk tolerance, memory, and value function are limited.

The job is not to dump all information upward. The job is to translate complex reality into a format that helps the manager decide, authorize, and trust.

Good upward communication provides the right signal, controls expectations, exposes risk early, and asks for the specific decision or resource needed.

## Diagnostic Questions

For any complex task, ask:

- What is the real problem?
- What is in scope and out of scope?
- What is the smallest verifiable output?
- What is the biggest risk or unknown?
- Who needs to be aligned?
- How will we know if the work is still on track?
- What does the manager need to know in order to grant trust and autonomy?

The goal is not to sound sophisticated. The goal is to make reality more tractable.
